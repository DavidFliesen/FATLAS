FATLAS-001 Intelligence Briefing

Fat Accumulation Targeting and Localized Adipose Signaling

Research Question

Why does the body preferentially store fat around the abdomen in some people but not others?

Executive Summary

The FATLAS-001 research round identified six major biological pathways that appear to converge on visceral abdominal fat accumulation: insulin resistance, cortisol signaling, chronic inflammation, adipocyte dysfunction, aging-related hormonal shifts and senescence, and gut microbiome-host metabolic crosstalk.

Central Hypothesis

Preferential visceral fat accumulation may be driven by a self-perpetuating cycle involving altered adipocyte sensitivity to insulin and glucocorticoids, chronic low-grade inflammation, gut microbiome disruption, age-related hormonal changes, and genetic predisposition.

Top Pathways

Insulin resistance, local cortisol amplification through 11β-HSD1, chronic low-grade inflammation, adipocyte dysfunction, senescence, and microbiome-driven inflammatory signaling emerged as the major converging pathways.

Candidate Control Points

Potential research targets include improving insulin sensitivity, modulating local cortisol amplification, reducing adipose inflammation, strengthening gut barrier integrity, studying senescent cell signaling, and comparing visceral and subcutaneous adipose tissue behavior.

Major Knowledge Gaps

The biggest unresolved question is why visceral fat cells behave differently than subcutaneous fat cells, and what determines whether excess energy is stored around organs, under the skin, or in lower-body fat depots.

Next Research Round

FATLAS-002 should expand beyond visceral fat and compare visceral adipose tissue, abdominal subcutaneous adipose tissue, and gluteofemoral subcutaneous adipose tissue.
